Carrier 360

Lifecycle-Driven Mobile UX for a Revenue-Critical Logistics Marketplace

[ Carrier 360 — load board as primary home screen, lifecycle load cards, and full workflow screens ]

At a Glance

Role Senior UX Designer, Mobile Product Lead
Company J.B. Hunt Transportation
Timeline 2018–2024
Tools Figma · Miro · Adobe Creative Cloud
Key Outcomes Load board repositioned as primary home screen · Lifecycle-based load card architecture shipped · Search and filtering redesigned as primary freight discovery surface · Design system governance established across 30+ products · 70%+ enterprise design system adoption achieved

Overview

Carrier 360 is J.B. Hunt's core mobile marketplace connecting shippers and carriers across North America. It serves as the primary operational tool for tens of thousands of drivers handling real-time load discovery, booking, execution, and payment — supporting 175,000+ carriers under demanding on-the-road conditions where fast, reliable decision-making isn't a nice-to-have, it's the job.

As Senior UX Designer leading mobile product design, I spent six years evolving the experience to reduce friction in high-density workflows across multiple major releases.

The Problem

Carrier 360 grew organically — and it showed. Fragmented information architecture and inconsistent patterns had accumulated over time, creating a product that worked but worked against its users.

Drivers had to navigate multiple screens to track a single load across its full lifecycle — discovery, booking, execution, and payment — each stage living in a different part of the app. The cognitive load was high, the navigation was redundant, and the most important surface in the app was buried.

The load board — the primary revenue-generating surface where drivers find and book freight — required navigating through the home screen, into a menu, past nearly a dozen primary options, before a driver could even see available loads.

Old flow: Home → Menu → Load Board → Filters → Results

That's four steps before a driver can do the one thing they opened the app to do.

Constraints

  • Scale: 175,000+ carriers, tens of thousands of daily active users — changes had to ship without disrupting a platform people depend on for their livelihood
  • Incremental delivery: All changes rolled out across multiple releases aligned to engineering timelines and executive roadmap priorities — no big-bang redesigns
  • On-road context: Users are drivers, not desk workers. The interface had to function in demanding physical environments: one hand, moving, time-pressured
  • Executive visibility: The platform was revenue-critical at the Fortune 250 level — UX decisions required executive alignment and cross-functional coordination

Key UX Decisions

Repositioning the load board as the primary home screen

The single highest-impact decision of the engagement. Elevating the load board from buried navigation to the primary home screen eliminated four steps of overhead and aligned the app directly with user intent. Drivers open Carrier 360 to find loads. The app now opens to loads.

[ Load board repositioned as the primary home screen — replacing buried menu navigation with immediate load visibility ]

Lifecycle-based load card architecture

I mapped the complete load lifecycle across four stages — discovery, booking and confirmation, shipment execution, delivery and payment — and restructured the load card to surface contextual, stage-relevant information at each point. A driver looking at a load they haven't booked sees different information than a driver actively executing a delivery. The card adapts to where they are in the workflow rather than showing everything all the time.

This reduced cognitive load across high-density mobile workflows and improved decision clarity at each stage — particularly during time-sensitive moments on the road.

[ Lifecycle-based load cards — discovery, booking/confirmation, execution, and delivery/payment stages each surfacing stage-relevant information ]

Search and filtering architecture

The existing filtering system was insufficient for the complexity of freight matching. Drivers needed to filter by location, timing, route constraints, equipment type, and rate — quickly, reliably, and without leaving the load board. I redesigned search and filtering as a first-class surface, not an afterthought. It became the primary method for relevant load discovery and booking.

[ Search & filtering surface — filter by location, timing, route, equipment type, and rate without leaving the load board ]

Incremental modernization

Six years of changes didn't ship all at once. Each improvement was scoped, sequenced, and aligned to engineering constraints and executive priorities. This approach preserved platform stability while steadily raising the experience quality across multiple releases.

Enterprise Design System

Parallel to the Carrier 360 work, I co-led a CIO-sponsored enterprise design system initiative that extended well beyond the mobile product.

  • Directed 4–5 dedicated designers and partnered with principal engineering leadership
  • Built governance supporting 50+ designers across 30+ web and mobile products
  • Drove 70%+ adoption across web applications
  • Conducted a comprehensive mobile audit identifying a <10% mobile adoption baseline and documenting critical implementation gaps
  • Served as primary design authority for standards enforcement and executive cross-product reviews

The mobile audit findings directly informed the roadmap for bringing Carrier 360 and other mobile products into alignment with the enterprise system — a gap that existed precisely because mobile had been treated as secondary to web.

Strategic Navigation Initiative

Selected as mobile design lead for a CIO-sponsored navigation initiative developed in partnership with Google. Defined core UX flows and contributed to cross-company product discussions before organizational reprioritization halted the launch.

Results

  • Load board elevated to primary home screen — eliminating navigation overhead for the platform's most critical surface
  • Lifecycle-based load cards shipped across multiple releases — reducing cognitive load and improving decision clarity for drivers
  • Search and filtering redesigned as the primary freight discovery surface
  • Enterprise design system governance established across 30+ products with 70%+ web adoption
  • Mobile adoption gap identified and documented — creating the roadmap for future mobile system alignment
  • Platform stability maintained throughout six years of iterative modernization at Fortune 250 scale

What I'd Measure Next

  • Load booking rate by filter usage — do drivers who use the redesigned filtering system book at a higher rate than those who don't? This is the clearest signal of whether the search architecture is working
  • Time-to-book — from load board open to confirmed booking. Lifecycle card improvements should show up here as a reduction in time and navigation steps
  • Mobile design system adoption — the audit identified <10% baseline. Tracking adoption over subsequent releases would quantify the impact of the governance work
  • Stage-appropriate information accuracy — are drivers seeing the right information at the right lifecycle stage, or are there drop-off points that suggest the card architecture needs refinement?

Reflections

Six years on a single product teaches you things a series of shorter engagements can't. The most important lesson from Carrier 360: the highest-impact UX decisions are often the simplest ones that nobody had prioritized yet.

Moving the load board to the home screen wasn't technically complex. It was organizationally complex — it required alignment across product, engineering, and executive stakeholders at a Fortune 250 company. The design was the easy part. The systems thinking, stakeholder navigation, and incremental execution strategy were the actual work.

The lifecycle card architecture was the same story. The insight — that a driver booking a load needs different information than a driver delivering one — seems obvious in retrospect. Making it real across a platform of that scale, without breaking anything, across multiple release cycles, is the work that doesn't show up in a screenshot.
